# Company-Specific Interview Patterns (Expanded)

Curated notes on how interview loops typically differ across well-known
tech companies, based on commonly reported candidate experiences and
public engineering-blog descriptions of hiring processes. Treat specific
question examples as illustrative patterns, not leaked real questions.

## Large Search/Ads Company (Google-style)
- Loop structure: typically 4-5 rounds -- 2-3 coding/algorithms rounds,
  1 "Googleyness"/behavioral round, sometimes 1 system design round for
  mid+ levels.
- Coding rounds emphasize clean, correct code on a shared doc/whiteboard
  with no IDE assistance -- interviewers watch problem-solving process,
  not just the final answer.
- Strong signal: considering multiple approaches out loud before coding,
  discussing Big-O trade-offs unprompted, testing your own code with
  edge cases at the end.
- Behavioral round evaluates collaboration, ambiguity handling, and
  intellectual humility (e.g., "tell me about a time you changed your
  mind based on data").

## Large E-commerce/Cloud Company (Amazon-style)
- Heavily structured around leadership-principle-style behavioral
  questions in nearly every round, including technical ones -- expect a
  behavioral question even in a "coding" interview slot.
- Bar-raiser round: an interviewer from outside the hiring team, focused
  on calibration and culture fit, often the most rigorous round.
- STAR-format answers are explicitly expected; vague or team-attributed
  ("we did X") answers are a common rejection reason.
- Coding rounds are practical/OOP-design leaning at senior levels
  (e.g., "design a parking lot / rate limiter") alongside classic DSA.

## Large Enterprise Software Company (Microsoft-style)
- Loop often includes a dedicated "As Appropriate" (AA) round with a
  senior engineer or manager, which can be a stronger signal than
  earlier rounds.
- Coding rounds sometimes allow an IDE with basic autocomplete
  (verify per role) -- fewer "gotcha" puzzle questions, more
  practical implementation and debugging tasks.
- System design rounds for mid/senior roles often tie to Azure-style
  distributed systems concepts: scaling, reliability, service boundaries.
- Collaboration and growth-mindset framing shows up explicitly in
  behavioral questions (e.g., "tell me about feedback that was hard to hear").

## High-Growth Startups (general pattern)
- Fewer, more practical rounds: a take-home project or live pairing
  session is common instead of pure whiteboard DSA.
- Strong emphasis on "what have you shipped end-to-end" and ownership
  narratives over algorithmic puzzle-solving.
- Culture/founder-fit conversations are typically less structured but
  still heavily weighted in the final decision.

## Interview-Prep Takeaways Across Companies
- Always restate the problem and ask clarifying questions before diving
  into code or a behavioral answer -- this signal matters everywhere.
- Prepare 5-6 flexible STAR stories that can be adapted to different
  behavioral themes (conflict, failure, leadership, ambiguity) rather
  than memorizing one story per possible question.
- For system design, practice explicitly stating trade-offs rather than
  presenting one "correct" design -- interviewers are grading your
  reasoning process, not a single right answer.
